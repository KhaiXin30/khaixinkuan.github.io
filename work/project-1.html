Project 1

Music Sentiment and Socioeconomic Analysis & Modeling
Project Overview
Our team shared common interests in musics. We asked when society changes (economically and politically), do our popular songs shift with it? We looked for the signal inside two features: valence (how “positive” a song sounds) and danceability (how much it invites movement).
‍
Our guiding research questions were:
‍
- Can we predict valence using indicators like US GDP, birth/death rates, and the federal funds rate?
- Can we predict danceability using indicators like unemployment, election status, recession, S&P 500 returns, and death rate?
Method
Data pipeline
- Web-scraped Billboard Hot 100 data and matched songs to Spotify sentiment features using song title + artist as identifiers, resolving spelling/format inconsistencies.
- Merged in economic + contextual variables (GDP, unemployment, fed funds rate, S&P 500 returns, recession, election status, birth/death rates, incarceration rate).
- Standardized formats and handled missing data.
- Addressed heteroskedasticity and multicollinearity issues.
‍
Modeling & Evaluation
- Ran multivariate regressions to estimate relationships.
- Used OLS for statistical significance and interpretability (coefficients, p-values, R²).
- Ran 5-fold cross-validation (RMSE + normalized RMSE) to check generalizability.
Results
Valence
- U.S. GDP showed a statistically significant negative relationship with valence—consistent with the idea that higher prosperity may correspond with music that’s less upbeat / more introspective.
- Death rate and federal funds rate showed positive correlations with valence.
‍
Danceability
- Death rate  showed a negative correlation with danceability, suggesting societal stressors may align with less energetic, less rhythmic mainstream music.
- Some variables (elections, S&P500 returns, unemployment, recessions) weren’t significant
Limitations
- Using yearly aggregated data limited our ability to capture short-term shocks or delayed effects.
- Billboard/Spotify bias toward mainstream listening, missing demographic/platform diversity.
Takeaways
- The hardest part wasn’t about modeling, but building a trustworthy and high quality dataset across messy sources.
- Cross-validation is pivotal in making sure the model generalize well.
