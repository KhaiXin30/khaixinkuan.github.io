Sometimes at Cornell, I feel like a blade of tall grass in strong wind—pulled in every direction at once. There’s more to learn than you can ever finish, and each semester forces a decision: what do I lean into next?

Chapter 1: Building the data science foundation
[Report]
Learning the foundations and building it right. Collect data. Clean. Visualize. Model. Evaluate.
Project: Music Sentiment and Socioeconomic Analysis & Modeling  
‍Our team shared common interests in musics. We asked when society changes (economically and politically), do our popular songs shift with it? We looked for the signal inside two features: valence (how “positive” a song sounds) and danceability (how much it invites movement).
‍
Our guiding research questions were:
1. Can we predict valence using indicators like US GDP, birth/death rates, and the federal funds rate?
2. Can we predict danceability using indicators like unemployment, election status, recession, S&P 500 returns, and death rate?

Chapter 2: When data became language‍[Web][Github]
Somewhere along the way, I drifted toward words. Datasets turned into sentences. I learned cosine similarity, vectorizers, and the logic behind ranking. Then, I picked up just enough front end and back end to build a website that ranks UNESCO World Heritage sites with my friends.
Project: UNESCO World Heritage Semantic Search Engine
‍My friends and I teamed up to build our own information retrieval system. We wanted to retrieve UNESCO sites not just by word overlap, but by meaning: surfacing places that still feel relevant even when the phrasing doesn’t line up.
‍
To do that, we engineered an SVD-based semantic retrieval pipeline, compressing TF-IDF vectors into a latent space and ranking 1,100+ UNESCO site documents using cosine similarity to capture deeper relevance. To keep the system interpretable, we extracted the top five semantic tags for each query–document match (high-contributing terms from the reduced space), so users could see why a result was ranked highly. Finally, we built and deployed a Flask web app that handles live queries, runs semantic search, and returns dynamically ranked results in real time.

‍Chapter 3: Saying yes before I felt ready[Github]
My team were deciding how to predict financial losses from cybersecurity threats, and ridge regression offered a straightforward answer. A neural network offered something else: unfamiliarity. We chose the unfamiliar option, and the work became less about being correct and more about becoming capable.
Project: Predicting Cyberattack Financial Loss
‍Cyberattack Loss Prediction started because one of my teammates was deeply passionate about the topic. For me, it became more than a prediction project. It was the beginning of my neural network journey. We engineered an end-to-end pipeline (EDA, one-hot encoding, standard scaling, and data augmentation with SDV’s GaussianCopulaSynthesizer), then trained and compared ridge regression with a simple neural network. Finally, we deployed the model as a Streamlit web app for real-time inputs and predictions. The biggest takeaway wasn’t just performance—it was learning how to choose models thoughtfully, and pushing myself to learn new ones along the way.

‍Chapter 4: Where the questions became human‍[Youtube][Github]
If there’s one thing I love as much as making informed decisions, it’s understanding the mind. During add/drop, I found a newly introduced class: NeuroAI: Bridging Brains and Artificial Intelligence. For the first time, I stepped into the newest edge of AI—not just studying the latest models, but also understanding their limits, and asking whether neuroscience might point toward what’s missing. And strangely, the neural networks I had struggled with before returned with a different meaning: not just tools to use, but systems to question.
Project: Can Free Energy Principle Fix Catastrophic Forgetting?
This project explores the relationship between catastrophic forgetting in neural networks and the Free Energy Principle (FEP) from neuroscience. I study how a model begins to forget its original task when it learns a new one through LoRA adapters, and how that forgetting shows up as shifts in “surprise”—operationalized as free energy over the original labels.On top of this baseline, I test whether different regularizers—L1, L2, and a Free-Energy regularizer—can reduce forgetting while preserving the model’s ability to learn the new task.
